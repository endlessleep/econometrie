1.1 Simple Regression Model

Defining the Model

For variables \( x \) and \( y \), the simple linear regression model is defined as:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u $$

Variables \( x \) and \( y \) are randomly sampled and stored in a dataset.

  • \( y \) : Dependent variable
  • \( x \) : Independent variable
  • \( \beta_0 \) : Intercept parameter - measures how \( y \) varies when \( x = 0 \).
  • \( \beta_1 \) : Slope parameter - measures how \( y \) varies with changes in \( x \).
  • \( u \) : Error term or disturbance - represents unobserved effects that are not included in the regression equation.

Logarithmic Functional Forms

Some relationships are not linear by their nature. Logarithmic transformations allow for elasticity and semi-elasticity interpretations:

Model Dependent (y) Independent (x) Interpretation of \( \beta_1 \)
Level-level \( y \) \( x \) \( \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x \)
Level-log \( y \) \( \log(x) \) \( \Delta y = (\beta_1 / 100) \% \Delta x \)
Log-level \( \log(y) \) \( x \) \( \% \Delta y = (100 \cdot \beta_1) \Delta x \)
Log-log \( \log(y) \) \( \log(x) \) \( \% \Delta y = \beta_1 \% \Delta x \)

Expected Value & Mean Independence

As long as there is the intercept \( \beta_0 \) in the model, we can assume that: \( E(u) = 0 \).

Zero Conditional Mean Assumption

We assume that \( E(u|x) = E(u) \), meaning \( u \) is mean independent of \( x \). Combining this with \( E(u) = 0 \), we obtain:

$$ E(u|x) = 0 $$

This leads to the Population Regression Function (PRF):

$$ E(y|x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x $$

Method of Moments Estimation

This leads to two practically important implied equations used for the estimation of parameters \( \beta_0 \) and \( \beta_1 \):

  • Theoretical: \( E(u) = E(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x) = 0 \)
  • Theoretical: \( Cov(x,u) = E[x(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x)] = 0 \)

Estimator for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \)

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

From the first equation, we substitute sample moments for theoretical moments and solve for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \):

$$ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \frac{1}{n} \sum y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 \frac{1}{n} \sum 1 - \hat{\beta}_1 \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i = 0 \iff $$ $$ \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Estimator for \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x})} $$

From the second equation, we get (substitute for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \) and solve for \(\hat{\beta}_1 \)):

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - (\bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}) - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) - \hat{\beta}_1 \sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x}) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x})} $$

Alternative Notations of the OLS Slope Estimator \( \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \)

The OLS estimator is perfectly equivalent to the MM estimator above. Here are alternative ways \( \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \) can be written:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Knowing that \( \sum(x_i - \bar{x})\bar{y} = \bar{y} \sum(x_i - \bar{x}) = \bar{y} \cdot 0 = 0 \), we can factorize the numerator \( \sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y}) \):

$$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) = \sum (x_i - \bar{x} + \bar{x}) (y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + \sum \bar{x}(y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + \bar{x}\sum(y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + 0 $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) $$

And knowing that \( \sum x_i \bar{x} = \bar{x} \sum x_i = n\bar{x}^2 = \sum \bar{x}^2 \), we can rewrite the denominator \( \sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x}) \):

$$ \sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x}) = \sum x_i^2 - \sum x_i \bar{x} $$ $$ = \sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2 = \sum x_i^2 - 2n\bar{x}^2 + n\bar{x}^2 $$ $$ = \sum x_i^2 - 2\bar{x}\sum x_i + \sum \bar{x}^2 $$ $$ = \sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum y_i (x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

To go from \( \sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y}) \) to \( \sum y_i(x_i - \bar{x}) \), we simply expand and refactor:

$$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) = \sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \bar{y} $$

Since \( \sum x_i \bar{y} = n\bar{x}\bar{y} = \sum \bar{x} y_i \):

$$ \sum x_i y_i - \sum \bar{x} y_i = \sum y_i (x_i - \bar{x}) $$

1.2 OLS Properties

Algebraic Properties of OLS

1. Sum of residuals equals zero

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

The sum (or sample mean) of the OLS residuals is zero.

This follows from the first first-order condition (FOC) of the OLS minimization problem.

2. Residuals are orthogonal to the explanatory variable

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

The sample covariance between the explanatory variable \( x \) and the residuals is zero.

This follows from the second first-order condition (FOC) of the OLS minimization problem.

3. Observed values can be decomposed

Each observed value of \( y \) can be decomposed into a fitted value and a residual:

$$ y_i = \hat{y}_i + \hat{u}_i $$

The fitted values and residuals are orthogonal:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{y}_i \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

Since \( \hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_i \), it is a linear combination of constants and \( x \), both of which are orthogonal to the residuals.

4. Means of observed and fitted values are equal

$$ \bar{y} = \overline{\hat{y}} $$

Equivalently: \( \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{y}_i \)

Since \( y_i = \hat{y}_i + \hat{u}_i \), summing both sides gives \( \sum y_i = \sum \hat{y}_i + \sum \hat{u}_i \). Because \( \sum \hat{u}_i = 0 \), we get \( \sum y_i = \sum \hat{y}_i \).

5. The point (\( \bar{x},\bar{y} \)) lies on the OLS regression line

Regression line: \( \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x \)

If \( x = \bar{x} \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = \bar{y} $$

Therefore the point \( (\bar{x},\bar{y}) \) always lies on the regression line.

We know that \( \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} \). Rearranging this formula directly yields \( \bar{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} \).

Sums of Squares

Term Name Formula Measures...
SST Total Sum of Squares \( \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 \) Total variation of \( y \) around its mean.
SSE Explained Sum of Squares \( \sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2 \) Variation explained by the regression model.
SSR Residual Sum of Squares \( \sum \hat{u}_i^2 \) Unexplained variation.

Decomposition of variance

$$ SST = SSE + SSR $$

Total variation = explained variation + unexplained variation.

Write \( y_i - \bar{y} = (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y}) + \hat{u}_i \). Square both sides and sum to get SST. The cross product term \( 2\sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})\hat{u}_i \) equals zero by the algebraic properties of OLS residuals discussed above.

Goodness of Fit

Coefficient of determination (\( R^2 \))

$$ R^2 = \frac{SSE}{SST} = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST} $$

Interpretation

\( R^2 \) measures the fraction of the sample variation in \( y \) explained by \( x \).

  • Range: \( 0 \leq R^2 \leq 1 \)
  • It is sometimes reported in percentages.
  • Important property: \( R^2 \) cannot decrease when adding an explanatory variable.
  • Threshold values depend on: the discipline, the field of economics, and the type of data.

Unbiasedness of OLS

The unbiasedness of the OLS estimator relies on the SLR (Simple Linear Regression) assumptions.

SLR.1 Linear in parameters
\( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u \)
\( \beta_0 \) is the population intercept, \( \beta_1 \) is the population slope. The presence of the intercept implies \( E(u) = 0 \).
SLR.2 Random sampling
We observe a random sample of size \( n \) drawn from the population.
SLR.3 Variation in the explanatory variable
The sample values of \( x \) are not all identical: \( \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \neq 0 \).
SLR.4 Zero conditional mean
\( E(u|x) = 0 \)
This implies \( Cov(x,u) = 0 \) and \( E(u) = 0 \).

Unbiasedness result

Under assumptions SLR.1–SLR.4,

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 \quad \text{and} \quad E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 $$

Therefore, \( \hat{\beta}_0 \) and \( \hat{\beta}_1 \) are unbiased estimators.

Start from the OLS estimator:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Using the linear model:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i $$

Substitute:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i)(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Expand:

$$ = \beta_0 \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} + \beta_1 \frac{\sum x_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} + \frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Use properties:

$$ \sum (x_i-\bar{x}) = 0 $$ $$ \sum x_i(x_i-\bar{x}) = \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 $$

Thus:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Take expectations conditional on \( x \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})E(u_i)}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Using the assumption \( E(u_i|x_i)=0 \) we obtain:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1)=\beta_1 $$

Starting from the population model and the zero conditional mean assumption:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i $$ $$ E(u_i \mid x_i) = 0 $$

Recall the OLS identity for the intercept:

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Take expectations (conditionally on \( x \)):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = E(\bar{y}) - \bar{x}E(\hat{\beta}_1) $$

Now, compute \( E(\bar{y}) \):

$$ \bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum y_i \implies E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum E(y_i) $$

From the population model, \( E(y_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i \). Substituting this:

$$ E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i) $$ $$ E(\bar{y}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x} $$

Substitute this result back into the expectation of \( \hat{\beta}_0 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = (\beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x}) - \bar{x}E(\hat{\beta}_1) $$

Finally, using the unbiasedness of the slope \( E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x} - \beta_1 \bar{x} $$ $$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 $$

Variance of the OLS Estimators

Homoskedasticity assumption

SLR.5 Homoskedasticity: The variance of the error term is constant:

$$ Var(u|x) = \sigma^2 $$

This means the variance of the errors does not depend on \( x \). Under this assumption: \( Var(y|x) = \sigma^2 \).

Opposite case: Heteroskedasticity.

Under SLR.1–SLR.5:

Variance of \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Variance of \( \hat{\beta}_0 \)

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_0) = \frac{\sigma^2 \sum x_i^2}{n \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Start from the rewritten OLS estimator:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Since the variance of a constant is zero:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1) = Var\left(\frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}\right) $$

Factor out the constant denominator:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} Var\left(\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})\right) $$

Using the variance of a sum:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum Var\left(u_i(x_i-\bar{x})\right) $$

Since \( x_i \) are constants:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 Var(u_i) $$

Using \( Var(u_i)=\sigma^2 \):

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 \sigma^2 $$

Factor out \( \sigma^2 \):

$$ = \sigma^2 \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} $$

Simplify:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1)=\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Estimating the Error Variance

The variance of the error term \( \sigma^2 \) is not observed and must be estimated. The unbiased estimator is:

$$ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n-2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{u}_i^2 $$

We divide by \( n-2 \) because we lose two degrees of freedom. Two restrictions exist on residuals: \( \sum \hat{u}_i = 0 \) and \( \sum x_i \hat{u}_i = 0 \).

Standard errors

  • Standard error of the regression: \( \hat{\sigma} \).
  • Standard error of \( \hat{\beta}_1 \):
    $$ se(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}} $$
    This is used to construct test statistics and confidence intervals.

Regression through the Origin

In rare cases we impose: \( \beta_0 = 0 \). The model becomes:

$$ y = \beta_1 x + u $$

OLS estimator:

$$ \tilde{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i y_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2} $$

Important Warning

If in reality \( \beta_0 \neq 0 \), then \( \tilde{\beta}_1 \) is biased. Therefore regression through the origin is rarely used in practice.

Important note: The definition of \( R^2 \) changes for regressions through the origin.

1.3 Multiple Regression Model

Model with \( k \) Independent Variables

In multiple regression, we extend the simple model to include multiple explanatory variables:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \dots + \beta_k x_k + u $$

Key Assumption

$$ E(u \mid x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k) = 0 $$

Meaning: The error term is uncorrelated with all explanatory variables (Zero Conditional Mean). This ensures that estimators are unbiased.

Matrix Notation

We represent the model using matrices for cleaner derivations:

$$ Y = X\beta + u $$

Vector \( Y \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{pmatrix} $$
$$ = $$

Matrix \( X \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_{11} & \dots & x_{1k} \\ 1 & x_{21} & \dots & x_{2k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{n1} & \dots & x_{nk} \end{pmatrix} $$

Vector \( \beta \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_k \end{pmatrix} $$
$$ + $$

Vector \( u \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_n \end{pmatrix} $$

Matrix Calculus Reference

Essential derivatives for MCO matrix proofs:

Expression Derivative w.r.t. \( \beta \)
\( a^T \beta \) or \( \beta^T a \) \( a \)
\( \beta^T \beta \) \( 2\beta \)
\( \beta^T A \beta \) (A symmetric) \( 2A\beta \)
\( \beta^T A \beta \) (A non-symmetric) \( (A + A^T)\beta \)
\( (A\beta)^T (A\beta) \) \( 2A^T A \beta \)

OLS Estimator (Matrix Form)

The vector of OLS estimates is given by the Normal Equations:

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y $$

The vector \(\hat{\beta}\) contains the \((k+1)\) estimated coefficients (intercept + \(k\) slopes).

Start with residuals: \( \hat{u} = Y - X\hat{\beta} \). Minimize the Sum of Squared Residuals (SSR):

$$ SSR = \hat{u}^T \hat{u} = (Y - X\hat{\beta})^T (Y - X\hat{\beta}) $$

Expand the quadratic form:

$$ SSR = Y^T Y - Y^T X\hat{\beta} - \hat{\beta}^T X^T Y + \hat{\beta}^T X^T X \hat{\beta} $$ $$ SSR = Y^T Y - 2\hat{\beta}^T X^T Y + \hat{\beta}^T X^T X \hat{\beta} $$

Take the derivative with respect to \( \hat{\beta} \) (F.O.C.):

$$ \frac{\partial SSR}{\partial \hat{\beta}} = -2X^T Y + 2(X^T X)\hat{\beta} = 0 $$

Solve for \( \hat{\beta} \):

$$ (X^T X)\hat{\beta} = X^T Y \implies \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y $$

Interpretation of Coefficients

Intercept (\( \hat{\beta}_0 \)): The predicted value of \( y \) when all independent variables are zero (\( x_1 = x_2 = \dots = x_k = 0 \)).

Slope Coefficients (\( \hat{\beta}_j \)): Measures the partial effect of \( x_j \) on \( y \).

$$ \Delta \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_j \Delta x_j $$

This represents the change in \( y \) for one unit change in \( x_j \), holding all other variables fixed (ceteris paribus).

Multiple Linear Regression Assumptions

Gauss-Markov assumptions for unbiasedness in multiple regression:

MLR.1 Linear in Parameters
\( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \dots + \beta_k x_k + u \).
MLR.2 Random Sampling
We observe a random sample reflecting the population.
MLR.3 No Perfect Collinearity
No independent variable is a perfect linear combination of others. \( X \) has full column rank.
MLR.4 Zero Conditional Mean
\( E(u \mid x_1, \dots, x_k) = 0 \). This implies \( Cov(x_j, u) = 0 \).

Unbiasedness of OLS

Under assumptions MLR.1 through MLR.4, the OLS estimator is unbiased:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}) = \beta $$

This means \( E(\hat{\beta}_j) = \beta_j \) for all \( j = 0, \dots, k \).

Start with the OLS estimator and substitute \( Y = X\beta + u \):

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T (X\beta + u) $$

Distribute the terms:

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} (X^T X) \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u $$ $$ \hat{\beta} = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u $$

Take expectations conditional on \( X \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T E(u \mid X) $$

Using MLR.4 \( E(u \mid X) = 0 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = \beta + 0 = \beta $$

Model Misspecification

Analyzing the impact of model choice on estimator properties.

Overspecified Model

Adding irrelevant variables (where true \(\beta_j = 0\)).

Result: MCO remains unbiased, but variance may increase.

Underspecified Model

Omitting a relevant variable (Omitted Variable Bias).

Result: MCO estimators become biased if \( Corr(x_1, x_{omitted}) \neq 0 \).

Bias in misspecified models: The true population model is:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + u_i $$

But we estimate a simple regression with only one explanatory variable:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + u_i $$

The OLS estimator is:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Substitute the true model for \( y_i \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + u_i) (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$ $$ = \frac{ \beta_0 \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \beta_1 \sum x_{i1}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \beta_2 \sum x_{i2}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \sum u_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) }{\sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Using properties of sample means: \( \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) = 0 \) and \( \sum x_{i1}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) = \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2 \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i2}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Taking expectations:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \frac{\sum (x_{i2}-\bar{x}_2)(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Summary of Omitted Variable Bias

Relation \( \beta_2 > 0 \) \( \beta_2 < 0 \)
\( Corr(x_1, x_2) > 0 \) Positive Bias Negative Bias
\( Corr(x_1, x_2) < 0 \) Negative Bias Positive Bias

Variance of OLS Estimators

To derive the variance, we add the final Gauss-Markov assumption:

MLR.5 Homoskedasticity

$$ Var(u \mid X) = \sigma^2 I_n $$

The variance of residuals is constant across all observations.

Under MLR.1–MLR.5, the variance-covariance matrix of \( \hat{\beta} \) is:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}) = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} $$

Start with the linear expression: \( \hat{\beta} = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u \).

The variance is given by:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = Var((X^T X)^{-1} X^T u \mid X) $$

Using the property \( Var(Az) = A Var(z) A^T \):

$$ = [(X^T X)^{-1} X^T] Var(u \mid X) [(X^T X)^{-1} X^T]^T $$ $$ = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T (\sigma^2 I_n) X (X^T X)^{-1} $$ $$ = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} (X^T X) (X^T X)^{-1} $$ $$ = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} $$

Estimating Variance & Standard Errors

The unbiased estimator for \( \sigma^2 \) in multiple regression is:

$$ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{SSR}{n - k - 1} $$

Degrees of Freedom (df): \( n - (k+1) \). We subtract the number of estimated parameters.

Standard Errors of individual coefficients:

$$ se(\hat{\beta}_j) = \hat{\sigma} \sqrt{ [(X^T X)^{-1}]_{jj} } $$

Alternatively: \( Var(\hat{\beta}_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{SST_j(1 - R_j^2)} \), where \( R_j^2 \) is the R-squared from regressing \( x_j \) on all other independent variables.

1.4 Multiple Regression Analysis: Inference

Gauss-Markov Theorem

Under assumptions MLR.1 through MLR.5 (Gauss-Markov assumptions), the OLS estimator is the BLUE:

$$ \text{B.L.U.E. = Best Linear Unbiased Estimator} $$
  • Linear: \(\hat{\beta}_j\) can be expressed as a linear function of \(y_i\): \(\hat{\beta}_j = \sum w_{ij} y_i \).
  • Best: Within the class of linear unbiased estimators, OLS has the minimum variance (it is efficient).

For simple regression, the OLS estimator for the slope is:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x}) y_i}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \sum w_i y_i $$

where the weights are \( w_i = \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{\sum (x_k - \bar{x})^2} \). Since \(w_i\) only depends on \(x\), \(\hat{\beta}_1\) is a linear function of \(y\).

Normality Assumption (MLR.6)

To perform statistical inference (tests and confidence intervals), we need to know the distribution of the error term:

MLR.6 Normality

$$ u \sim N(0, \sigma^2) $$

The error \(u\) is independent of \(x\) and follows a Normal Distribution.

Classical Linear Model (CLM): Assumptions MLR.1 to MLR.6 together are called the CLM assumptions.

Under CLM, the OLS estimator is BUE (Best Unbiased Estimator), which is even stronger than BLUE.

Normal Sampling Distribution

Under MLR.1–MLR.6, the OLS estimators follow a normal distribution:

$$ \hat{\beta}_j \sim N\left( \beta_j, Var(\hat{\beta}_j) \right) $$

Standardizing gives us the standard normal result (if \(\sigma\) was known):

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{sd(\hat{\beta}_j)} \sim N(0, 1) $$

However, in practice we must estimate \(\sigma\), leading us to the \(t\)-distribution.

Hypothesis Testing (t-test)

Under CLM assumptions, the standardized estimator follows a \(t\)-distribution:

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} \sim t_{n-k-1} $$

t-ratio for \(H_0: \beta_j = 0\):

$$ t_{\hat{\beta}_j} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

We reject \(H_0\) if \(|t| > t_{critical}\) or if \(p\text{-value} < \alpha\).

One-sided Test

\(H_1: \beta_j > 0\) (or \(< 0\))
Focus on one tail.

Two-sided Test

\(H_1: \beta_j \neq 0\)
Focus on both tails.

Confidence Intervals

A \((1-\alpha)\) confidence interval for \(\beta_j\) is constructed as:

$$ \hat{\beta}_j \pm t_{n-k-1, 1-\alpha/2} \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

Rule of thumb (95% CI): For large samples (\(df > 120\)), the critical value is approx. \(2\).

If the CI contains \(0\), we cannot reject the null hypothesis \(H_0: \beta_j = 0\) at the \(\alpha\) significance level.

Economic vs. Statistical Significance

Statistical Significance

Is the \(p\)-value small enough? Is the effect different from zero purely due to chance?

Economic Significance

Is the magnitude of \(\beta_j\) large enough to matter in the real world?

Caution: A variable can be statistically significant but economically irrelevant (very small effect), or vice-versa (large effect but high standard error).

Testing Linear Combinations

Sometimes we want to test if two parameters are equal: \(H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2\).

The t-statistic:

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_2}{se(\hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_2)} $$

Define \(\theta = \beta_1 - \beta_2\). The null is \(H_0: \theta = 0\).

Substitute \(\beta_1 = \theta + \beta_2\) into the original model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + (\theta + \beta_2)x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$ $$ y = \beta_0 + \theta x_1 + \beta_2(x_1 + x_2) + u $$

By defining a new variable \(x^* = x_1 + x_2\), we can estimate the model and the \(t\)-ratio for \(x_1\) will directly test our hypothesis.

🎯 t-test (one-sided, right) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j \le \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j > \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } t > c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(t > c \mid H_0) = \alpha $$ $$ P(t < c) = 1 - \alpha $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( t_{obs} \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 t-test (one-sided, left) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j \ge \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j < \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } t < c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;\alpha} = -t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(t < c \mid H_0) = \alpha $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;\alpha} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( t_{obs} \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 t-test (two-sided) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j = \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j \neq \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } |t| > c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(-c < t < c \mid H_0) = 1 - \alpha $$ $$ P(t < -c \ \text{or} \ t > c) = \alpha $$ $$ P(t < -c) = P(t > c) = \frac{\alpha}{2} $$ $$ P(t < c) = 1 - \frac{\alpha}{2} $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( |t_{obs}| \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 Confidence Interval

$$ \hat{\beta}_j - t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) \;\le\; \beta_j \;\le\; \hat{\beta}_j + t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_j \;\mp\; t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation for the critical value \(t\).

To verify, we can also compare \(\beta_{j,0}\) to the confidence interval: if it lies within the interval, this confirms that we do not reject \(H_0\).

🔍 \(-c < t < c\)

$$ -c < \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} < c $$
$$ -c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) < \hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j < c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_j - c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) < \beta_j < \hat{\beta}_j + c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

🧪 Method: F-Test for Joint Hypothesis

When to use F-Test?

Use the F-test when you want to test multiple linear restrictions simultaneously (e.g., $H_0: \beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0$). The $t$-test is only for a single hypothesis.

1. Methodology (R vs UR)

Compare two versions of the model:

  • Unrestricted (UR): The full model with all variables ($k$ independent variables).
  • Restricted (R): The model after imposing $H_0$ ($q$ variables are removed or restricted).

Logic: $SSR_R \ge SSR_{UR}$ always. We test if the increase in $SSR$ is significant.

2. Hypotheses

$H_0$: $\beta_{k-q+1} = \dots = \beta_k = 0$

(The $q$ variables have no effect on $y$)

$H_1$: At least one $\beta_j \neq 0$

3. Test Statistics (Forms)

Standard / SSR Form

$$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_{UR})/q}{SSR_{UR}/(n-k-1)} $$

R-Squared Form

$$ F = \frac{(R^2_{UR} - R^2_R)/q}{(1 - R^2_{UR})/(n-k-1)} $$

4. Degrees of Freedom

  • $q$: Number of restrictions (Variables dropped).
  • $n-k-1$: Degrees of freedom of the Unrestricted model.
  • Distribution: $F \sim F_{q, \;n-k-1}$

5. Decision Rule

Compare your $F$-statistic to the Critical Value ($c$) from the table:

If $F_{stat} > c \Rightarrow$ Reject $H_0$

Conclusion: The restrictions are rejected. The dropped variables are jointly significant.

Case Study: Overall Significance

This checks if any of your variables have explanatory power.

  • $H_0$: $\beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0, \dots, \beta_k = 0$ (All zero except intercept)
  • Restricted Model $R^2$: $R^2_R = 0$
  • Simplified Formula:
$$ F = \frac{R^2 / k}{(1 - R^2) / (n-k-1)} $$

1.1 Functional Forms: Interpretations & The "Log-Trap"

Model Regression Form Interpretation Rule
Level-Log $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(x) + u$ A 1% change in $x$ leads to a $(\beta_1 / 100)$ unit change in $y$.
Log-Level $\ln(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u$ A 1 unit change in $x$ leads to a $(100 \beta_1)\%$ change in $y$.
Log-Log $\ln(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(x) + u$ Elasticity: A 1% change in $x$ leads to a $\beta_1\%$ change in $y$.
Quadratic $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 x^2 + u$ $dy/dx = \beta_1 + 2\beta_2 x$. Turning point: $x^* = -\beta_1 / 2\beta_2$.
The Log-Selection Bias (Crucial Reasoning)

The Rule: Log transformations require strictly positive values ($x, y > 0$).
The Trap: If your dataset contains zeros or negatives (e.g., zero-wage workers or loss-making firms), they are automatically dropped.
The Bias: Since those dropped observations are NOT random (they share a specific characteristic like "being poor" or "failing"), you have violated MLR.2 (Random Sampling). This creates Sample Selection Bias, and your estimated $\beta$ will not represent the whole population.

Why Logs? To reduce skewness, handle extreme outliers, and allow % change (elasticity) interpretation.

💡 Quadratic Terms in OLS ($x^2$)

🔍 The Model
$$y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 x^2 + u$$

👉 Rule 1: The effect of $x$ on $y$ is NOT constant.
👉 Rule 2: It depends entirely on the current level of $x$.

⚡ The Marginal Effect

Take the derivative with respect to $x$:

$\frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = \beta_1 + 2\beta_2 x$

The slope of the curve changes as you move along the $x$-axis!

📉 Concave vs Convex
  • $\beta_2 < 0$: Concave (Inverted U). The effect eventually diminishes.
  • $\beta_2 > 0$: Convex (U-shaped). The effect accelerates.
🎯 Special Case

If $y = \beta_0 + \beta_2 x^2$:
Then $\frac{\text{d}y}{\text{d}x} = 2\beta_2 x$.
👉 The effect depends entirely on $x$.

⚡ Exam Reflex: "With quadratic terms, the effect of $X$ varies with its level. It is no longer a straight line!"

🔵 1.2 Scaling (Changing Units)

👉 Case 1: Level Model (Normal)

If $Y$ is rescaled (multiplied by $c$):

  • Both intercept ($\hat{\beta}_0$) and slope ($\hat{\beta}_1$) are multiplied by $c$.
  • Standard errors are also multiplied by $c$.
  • $t$-stats and $R^2$ remain identical.

If $X$ is rescaled (multiplied by $c$):

  • The slope ($\hat{\beta}_1$) is divided by $c$.
  • The intercept ($\hat{\beta}_0$) is NOT affected. ✅
  • Standard Errors are divided by $c$.
  • $t$-stats and $R^2$ remain identical.

🌟 The Scaling Intuition:

Rescaling is just changing the ruler (meters to km). The statistical significance and the fit ($R^2$) are invariant because they measure "quality" and "signal", which stay the same regardless of units.

👉 Case 2: Model with LOGS

$\ln(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(x) + u$
If you multiply $x$ by 1000 : $\ln(1000x) = \ln(x) + \ln(1000)$

Final Result:

  • The coefficient $\hat{\beta}$ does NOT change ✅
  • The standard error does NOT change
  • The $t$-stat does NOT change
  • Only the intercept changes (it absorbs the constant)

Intuition: In log models, we measure percentages. A percentage is naturally stable, regardless of the unit (grams or tons).

👉 Case 3: Data Shifting (Translation)

What happens if we add or subtract a constant to $X$ or $Y$? (i.e., shifting the whole data cloud vertically or horizontally).

🌟 The Invariance Rule:

  • The Slope estimate ($\hat{\beta}_1$) is UNAFFECTED. ✅
  • Only the Intercept ($\hat{\beta}_0$) changes to absorb the repositioning.

Intuition: A shift doesn't change the "tilt" or the correlation between variables; it only changes the baseline.

⚡ Exam Reflex: "Shifting data (adding a constant) does NOT change the slope. Only the intercept is affected."

🔴 1.3 OVB (Omitted Variable Bias)

OVB stands for Omitted Variable Bias.

It occurs when your model is incorrect because you are missing a crucial variable ($X_2$) that "works in the shadows".

Why is it serious?

Your variable $X_1$ is forced to "steal" the effect of the missing variable. Your result is therefore polluted.
👉 Violation: We break the MLR.4 assumption (Exogeneity). Causal interpretation is dead. 💀

📊 Bias Sign Logic Table ($\beta_2 \cdot \tilde{\delta}_1$)
Corr($X_1, X_2$) > 0 Corr($X_1, X_2$) < 0
$\beta_2 > 0$ POSITIVE BIAS (+)
(Upward Bias)		 NEGATIVE BIAS (-)
(Downward Bias)
$\beta_2 < 0$ NEGATIVE BIAS (-)
(Downward Bias)		 POSITIVE BIAS (+)
(Upward Bias)
🔵 The Causal Bridge: Ceteris Paribus

For OLS to uncover a Causal Effect (the "True Relationship"), we require the model to explicitly include all relevant factors to hold them fixed (Ceteris Paribus).

👉 The Logic: If an important variable is missing from the model but affects $Y$, it stays in the error term ($u$).
👉 The Danger: If this missing variable is correlated with your $X$, then $E[u \mid X] \neq 0$.
👉 Result: Your estimated coefficient is Biased and does NOT represent the true effect. You can no longer say "all else being equal".

Exam Reflex: "We include control variables not because we care about them, but to clean the error term $u$ and satisfy the Exogeneity assumption."

⚠️ Bias Direction vs. Over/Underestimation

The Trap: "Upward Bias" does NOT always mean "Overestimation". It depends on the sign of your coefficient $\beta_1$!

Case A: $\beta_1 > 0$ (Positive effect)
  • 🔼 Upward: Overestimation
  • 🔽 Downward: Underestimation
Case B: $\beta_1 < 0$ (Negative effect)
  • 🔼 Upward: Underestimation (closer to 0)
  • 🔽 Downward: Overestimation (further from 0)

🌟 The 2-Step Exam Strategy:

  1. Find the Bias Direction first (Sign of $\beta_2 \times$ Sign of Corr).
  2. Check the Sign of $\beta_1$ to decide if you are over or underestimating.

👉 "Bias direction concerns the value of the estimate. Over/Underestimation concerns the magnitude relative to reality."

💡 1.4 Inference & Diagnostics (Deep Dive)

🔵 Multicollinearity & VIF

Definition: Occurs when one explanatory variable is highly correlated with other regressors.
👉 Intuition: "Too many variables are telling the same story."

🔴 Relation to MLR.3 (Gauss-Markov)

Perfect Multicollinearity: (e.g., $x_2 = 2x_1$) VIOLATES MLR.3. OLS cannot be computed (Matrix is singular).
High Multicollinearity: SATISFIES MLR.3. It is allowed, but comes at a price!

⚠️ Consequences on OLS:
  • ✅ No Bias: Estimators are still centered on the truth.
  • ❌ Precision Loss: Variance of estimators $(\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}))$ explodes.
  • ❌ High SE: Standard Errors become very large.
  • ❌ Low t-stats: Variables look "Insignificant" because the noise drowns out the signal.
🎯 Detection & Thresholds
1. Simple Correlation:
Examine correlation between $x_i$ and $x_j$. If $> 75\text{--}80\%$, multicollinearity is likely a concern.
2. VIF (Variance Inflation Factor):
$VIF_j = 1 / (1 - R_j^2)$, where $R_j^2$ is from regressing $x_j$ on all other $x$.
VIF = 1
Ideal (No Correlation)
VIF > 5
Potential concern
VIF > 10
Severe Problem! 🔥
Multicollinearity: Remedies

1. Do nothing: Best to avoid potential Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) if the estimates are still reliable/significant.

2. Drop a redundant variable: Only if the variable is not theoretically essential for the model.

3. Transform variables: Use ratios, sums, or thresholds (dummies) if variables are theoretically essential.

4. Increase Sample size ($N \uparrow$): More data reduces the variance and the standard error of OLS estimators.

Ref: Wooldridge (2012), 3.4 "Multicollinearity".

🔵 Heteroskedasticity (MLR.5 Violation)

Definition: Since $\text{Var}(Y \mid X) = \text{Var}(u \mid X)$, if the variance of $Y$ increases with $X$, the error term is heteroskedastic.

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(Y \mid X) = \text{Var}(u \mid X)}$$
👉 Pourquoi ? (The Logic)

Si $X$ est fixé, alors on a :

$$ Y = \text{constante} + u $$

👉 Seule l’erreur varie $\rightarrow$ toute la variance vient de $u$.

✅ Homoskédasticité :

$\text{Var}(u \mid X) = \sigma^2$ (Constante)

❌ Hétéroscédasticité :

$\text{Var}(u \mid X)$ dépend de $X$

🔴 Relation to MLR.5 (Gauss-Markov)

Heteroskedasticity VIOLATES MLR.5 (Homoskedasticity).

⚠️ Consequences:
  • ✅ Estimates: Still Unbiased and Consistent.
  • ❌ Efficiency: OLS is no longer BLUE (it's not the best).
  • ❌ Inference: Standard Errors are WRONG. Classic $t$ and $F$ tests are invalid.

👉 Final Remedy: Use WHITE Robust Standard Errors. They correct the inference without changing the coefficients.

🔵 Significance vs R² (The Deep Logic)

Do NOT confuse them: Significance measures "Is the effect real?", while $R^2$ measures "How much do we explain?".

📉 Low R² / Significant β

Interpretation: The variable has a real, detectable effect on $Y$.
👉 BUT, $Y$ depends on many other factors not in the model.
👉 Example: Education and Wages. Education matters, but luck and talent explain most wage variation.

📈 High R² / Insignificant β

Interpretation: The model fits the data perfectly as a whole.
👉 BUT, you can't tell who did what.
👉 Usually caused by Multicollinearity (variables move together).

⚡ Exam Reflex:

"Significance is about detection. $R^2$ is about explanation. A low $R^2$ is NOT a death sentence for your research!"

MODULE 2: Theoretical Powerhouse

Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)

Goal: Choose $\beta_0, \beta_1$ to make errors as small as possible.

We start with the model: $y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i$
We define predicted values: $\hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_i$
And residuals: $\hat{u}_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i = y_i - (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i)$

Minimize the Sum of Squared Residuals (SSR): $$S(\beta_0,\beta_1) = \sum (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)^2$$ Take derivatives (FOCs) and set to zero: $$\frac{\partial S}{\partial \beta_0} = -2 \sum (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) = 0$$ $$\frac{\partial S}{\partial \beta_1} = -2 \sum x_i (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i) = 0$$ Solving for $\hat{\beta}_1$ gives the Covariance/Variance form: $$\hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$ And the intercept: $\hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}$

Method of Moments (MM)

Goal: Force the sample data to respect the model's theoretical logic.

We start with the assumption: $E(u_i \mid x_i) = 0$
This implies two conditions: $E(u_i) = 0$ and $E(x_i u_i) = 0$.

Sample Equivalents:
Replace $E[\cdot]$ with sample averaging $\frac{1}{n} \sum$:
1. $\frac{1}{n} \sum (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0$
2. $\frac{1}{n} \sum x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0$

Multiply by $n$ to get the Normal Equations: $$\sum y_i = n\hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \sum x_i$$ $$\sum x_i y_i = \hat{\beta}_0 \sum x_i + \hat{\beta}_1 \sum x_i^2$$ These are exactly the same equations as OLS!

💡 Variability of X and OLS Precision

🔍 The Core Result

The precision of the OLS estimator is directly tied to how much the independent variable varies.

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}$$
🧠 Intuition

The denominator $\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2$ measures the Information Content of $X$.
👉 The more $X$ varies, the more "signal" we have to detect how $Y$ responds.

🚀 High Variability in X:
Large denominator $\rightarrow$ Small $\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1)$
👉 Result: More precise estimates.

📉 Low Variability in X:
Small denominator $\rightarrow$ Large $\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1)$
👉 Result: Less precise estimates.

⚠️ The Extreme Case (Failure of MLR.3)

If $X$ is constant, $\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 = 0$.
The variance becomes infinite. OLS cannot compute $\hat{\beta}_1$. This is a violation of MLR.3 (No perfect collinearity with the constant).

💡 Variance of Composite Estimators ($\hat{\beta}_1 + \hat{\beta}_2$)

🔍 The Formula for $\hat{\theta}_1 = \hat{\beta}_1 + \hat{\beta}_2$

When testing the sum or difference of two coefficients, the variance is not just the sum of variances!

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1 + \hat{\beta}_2) = \text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1) + \text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_2) + 2\text{Cov}(\hat{\beta}_1, \hat{\beta}_2)$$

Or using Correlation ($Corr = Cov / \sqrt{V_1 V_2}$):
$$\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1 + \hat{\beta}_2) = \text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1) + \text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_2) + 2\text{Corr}(\hat{\beta}_1, \hat{\beta}_2) \sqrt{\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_1)\text{Var}(\hat{\beta}_2)}$$

🚀 The "Diversification" Logic

👉 Negative Correlation ($\text{Corr} < 0$):
The negative covariance piece reduces the total variance.
Result: You achieve a more stable/precise composite estimator.

⚡ Exam Reflex:

"When combining coefficients, the covariance term is crucial. A negative correlation between estimates improves the precision of their sum!"

Deep Difference to remember

  • OLS: Minimizes the sum of squared errors (Least Squares). It's an optimization problem.
  • MM: Imposes orthogonality conditions (Ortho = at a right angle/no correlation) between $x$ and $u$. It's a logic requirement.
  • 👉 Both result in the same Normal Equations and the same estimators.

Core Algebraic Properties (Always True)

  • 1. $\sum \hat{u}_i = 0$ (Residuals sum to zero)
  • 2. $\sum x_{ij} \hat{u}_i = 0$ (Zero correlation with $x$)
  • 3. $\bar{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}_1 + \dots$ (Regression line passes through means)
  • 4. $SST = SSR + SSE$ (Variation Decomposition)
Sum of Squares Formulas

$SST = \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2$ (Total variation)
$SSR = \sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2$ (Explained variation)
$SSE = \sum \hat{u}_i^2$ (Unexplained variation/Residuals)

Proof Basics: SLR vs MLR forms

Metric SLR (Single Linear Reg) MLR (Multiple / Matrix form)
Estimator $\frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}$ $(X'X)^{-1}X'y$
Unbiasedness $\beta_1 + \frac{\sum w_i u_i}{SST_x}$ $\beta + (X'X)^{-1}X'u$
Sampling Var $\sigma^2 / SST_x$ $\sigma^2 (X'X)^{-1}$
Linearity $\sum w_i y_i$ $Ay$, where $A = (X'X)^{-1}X'$

F-Test Statistic Forms

Use these to compare Restricted (R) vs Unrestricted (U) models:
SSE Form: $F = \frac{(SSE_R - SSE_U)/q}{SSE_U/(n-k-1)}$
R² Form: $F = \frac{(R^2_U - R^2_R)/q}{(1-R^2_U)/(n-k-1)}$

🔵 1. Why OLS is Unbiased: MLR 1 to 4

Unbiasedness ($E[\hat{\beta}] = \beta$) is the "Holy Grail". It means that on average, your estimator captures the True Population Parameter. To achieve this, you need 4 core assumptions. If even one is missing, your result is BIASED and misleading.

MLR.1: Linear in Parameters

👉 The Rule: The model MUST be linear in its parameters ($\beta$). You can have $x^2$ or $\log(x)$, but never $\beta^2$.
👉 Failure: If the true relationship between $Y$ and $X$ is nonlinear/complex and you force a straight line, your estimation will be permanently shifted from the truth.

MLR.2: Random Sampling

👉 The Rule: Your sample must be a faithful, random representation of the population.
👉 The Trap: If you use logs and automatically drop zero or negative observations (e.g., ignoring bankrupt firms), your sample is biased toward "survivors". This is Selection Bias.

MLR.3: No Perfect Collinearity

👉 The Rule: Explanatory variables must not be perfect linear combinations of each other.
👉 Intuition: OLS needs to see each $X$ vary independently. If $X_1$ and $X_2$ always move together in lockstep, the model is "blind" and the math literally fails (Matrix is non-invertible).

MLR.4: Zero Conditional Mean (Exogeneity)

👉 The Absolute Queen: $E[u \mid X] = 0$. Factors in the error term ($u$) must NOT be correlated with your $X$ variables.
👉 OVB Crisis: If you omit a variable that affects $Y$ AND is linked to $X$, your coefficient will "steal" that hidden variable's effect. The result is Endogenous and mathematically BIASED.

🔵 2. Precision, Efficiency & Inference: MLR 5 and 6

MLR.5: Homoskedasticity (Efficiency)

👉 The Rule: The variance of errors is constant ($\sigma^2$).
👉 Utility: This allows OLS to be BLUE (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator).

🔴 When MLR.5 is Violated (Heteroskedasticity):
  • ✅ Coefficients: Still centers correctly (Unbiased).
  • ⚠️ Efficiency: OLS is no longer optimal. It treats noisy observations and precise observations as equal, which is wasteful.
  • ❌ Inference: Standard Errors are WRONG. $t$-tests and $F$-tests are invalid. Conclusions about "Significance" are potentially false.

MLR.6: Normality (Exact Inference)

👉 Utility: Essential for $t$ and $F$ tests to be EXACT in small samples.

🌟 The Massive "Save" : Asymptotic Validity (CLT)

In Large Samples ($n > 30$), we don't worry about non-normality!
👉 Why? The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) ensures that the distribution of $\hat{\beta}$ converges to Normal regardless of the error distribution.
👉 The Result: Our tests become Asymptotically Valid.

🔵 3. Consistency (The bare minimum)

Consistency means that with an infinite sample, you hit the truth exactly.
👉 Condition: We only need Zero Correlation ($Cov(X, u) = 0$).
👉 This is less demanding than MLR.4, and we do not need normality or homoskedasticity for this property to hold.

🎓 Exam Logic: When is the t-test valid?

For a $t$-test (or $F$-test) to provide Exact Inference, we technically require ALL SIX assumptions (MLR.1 to MLR.6). If an assumption is broken, the test is likely "lying" to you.

The Universal Diagnostic Guide (Impact of Violations)
  • 1. Linearity (MLR.1): If the true population model is non-linear but you estimate a linear OLS, your estimators are Biased. The $t$-test is centered on the wrong value and is therefore invalid. ❌
  • 2. Random Sampling (MLR.2): If your sample is not random (e.g., selection bias), the results do not represent the population. Your estimated $\hat{\beta}$ is Biased, rendering the $t$-test meaningless for the population. ❌
  • 3. No Perfect Collinearity (MLR.3): High correlation (multicollinearity) does NOT violate MLR.3. The $t$-test remains technically valid, but it becomes very difficult to identify individual effects, leading to high standard errors. ⚠️
  • 4. Exogeneity (MLR.4): Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) or Endogeneity violates MLR.4. This makes your $\hat{\beta}$ Biased, which means your $t$-test is testing a "polluted" coefficient that does not reflect the true causal effect. ❌
  • 5. Homoskedasticity (MLR.5): Heteroskedasticity results in Invalid Standard Errors. Since the $t$-statistic is calculated using these standard errors, the entire $t$-test (and $F$-test) is invalid unless you use robust corrections. ❌
  • 6. Normality (MLR.6): If errors are non-normal, the $t$-test is Invalid for small samples. However, it remains "Asymptotically Valid" in large samples ($n > 30$) thanks to the Central Limit Theorem. ⚠️

🌟 The "Large Sample" Exception: With $n > 30$, the CLT "saves" you from MLR.6 (Normality). The $t$-test becomes Asymptotically Valid even without normality.

1.5 Testing Multiple Linear Restrictions & OLS Asymptotics

🎯 TL;DR: What to know for the Exam

  • 👉 F-test: Know the \(SSR\) vs \(R^2\) formulas by heart! Often asked to compute \(F\) from output variables.
  • 👉 Consistency (\(\text{plim}\)): You DO NOT need MLR.5 (homoskedasticity) or MLR.6 (normality) for consistency, only zero correlation.
  • 👉 Normality: Large sample + CLT \(\Rightarrow\) we no longer need the restrictive MLR.6 assumption for standard \(t\) and \(F\) tests.

Testing Exclusion Restrictions

We are interested in testing multiple hypotheses jointly (e.g., \(H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0\)). The \(t\)-statistic is not appropriate here, as we need to test the restrictions jointly.

Unrestricted vs. Restricted Models

Unrestricted Model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_3 x_3 + u $$

Restricted Model (assuming \(H_0\)):

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_3 x_3 + u $$
Key concept: \(SSR\) of the unrestricted model (\(SSR_U\)) will always be lower (or equal) to the \(SSR\) of the restricted model (\(SSR_R\)).

The \(F\) Test

To judge whether the restricted model performs significantly worse, we use the \(F\) statistic:

$$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_U)/q}{SSR_U/(n-k-1)} \;\sim\; F_{q, \;n-k-1} $$
  • \(q\): number of restrictions (numerator \(df\))
  • \(n-k-1\): degrees of freedom of the unrestricted model (denominator \(df\))
  • \(F \ge 0\) always!

\(SSR\) vs \(R^2\) form of the statistic

Using the definition \(R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST}\), we can substitute \(SSR = (1-R^2)SST\).

$$ F = \frac{(1 - R_R^2)SST - (1 - R_U^2)SST}{q} \div \frac{(1 - R_U^2)SST}{n-k-1} $$

\(SST\) cancels out:

$$ F = \frac{(R_U^2 - R_R^2)/q}{(1 - R_U^2)/(n-k-1)} $$

Overall Significance of a Regression

Econometric software constantly reports an overall \(F\) statistic. This tests the joint hypothesis that all parameters (except the intercept) are zero:

$$ H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \dots, \beta_k = 0 $$

In this case, \(R_R^2 = 0\) (no variables explain \(y\)) and \(q = k\). Thus:

$$ F = \frac{R^2 / k}{(1 - R^2) / (n-k-1)} $$

Testing General Linear Restrictions

Sometimes hypotheses are not just "excluding" variables, e.g., \(H_0: \beta_1 = 1, \beta_2 = 0\).

Example: CAPM Model

$$ r_{i,t} - r_{f,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i(r_{M,t} - r_{f,t}) + u_{i,t} $$

If \(H_0: \alpha_i = 0, \beta_i = 1\) (Efficient Market Hypothesis):

$$ r_{i,t} - r_{f,t} = (r_{M,t} - r_{f,t}) + u_{i,t} $$

This leaves nothing to estimate on the right-hand side! We simply compute:

$$ SSR_R = \sum (r_{i,t} - r_{M,t})^2 $$

Note: For general linear restrictions, we can only use the \(SSR\) form of the \(F\)-test (not \(R^2\)), because the dependent variable often changes between the restricted and unrestricted models.

OLS Asymptotics: Large Sample Inference

Consistency

Unbiasedness cannot always be achieved. Consistency is a minimal requirement: as \(n \to \infty\), the estimator collapses to the true parameter \(\beta_j\).

$$ \text{plim}(\hat{\beta}_j) = \beta_j \quad \text{under MLR.1 through MLR.4} $$

$$ \text{plim}(\hat{\beta}_1) - \beta_1 = \frac{Cov(x_1, u)}{Var(x_1)} $$

Asymptotic Normality and Efficiency

Normality of \(u\) (MLR.6) is a very strong assumption. Fortunately, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) comes to the rescue for large samples.

Under MLR.1 through MLR.5 (without MLR.6), as \(n \to \infty\):

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} \;\stackrel{a}{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}(0, 1) $$

This means we can use standard \(t\) and \(F\) statistics for large samples without assuming normally distributed errors. OLS estimators also become asymptotically efficient (lowest asymptotic variance).

Lagrange Multiplier Test

An alternative to the \(F\) test for multiple exclusion restrictions without assuming MLR.6. It tests if the omitted variables are correlated with the residuals of the restricted model.

6 Steps of the LM Test:
  1. State hypotheses: e.g., \(H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0\).
  2. Estimate the restricted model and save the residuals \(\tilde{u}\).
  3. Run an auxiliary regression: regress \(\tilde{u}\) on all independent variables (restricted + unrestricted) and obtain \(R_{\tilde{u}}^2\).
  4. Compute the test statistic: \(LM = n R_{\tilde{u}}^2\).
  5. Under \(H_0\), \(LM \sim \chi_q^2\) (Chi-square with \(q\) restrictions).
  6. Reject \(H_0\) if \(LM > c\) at the given significance level \(\alpha\).

DATA SCALING & FUNCTIONAL FORMS & MODEL COMPARISON

Effects of Data Scaling on OLS Statistics

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Why do we rescale data?

We mainly rescale data for reporting purposes:

  • to avoid too many decimals
  • to work with more intuitive units
  • to make coefficients easier to read and compare

Scaling an Independent Variable

Suppose we rescale \(x_1\) by a nonzero constant \(c\). Define a new variable \( x_1^* = c x_1 \). Starting from the original model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Since \( x_1 = x_1^*/c \), we can rewrite the model as:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \frac{\beta_1}{c}\,x_1^* + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Therefore, in the rescaled model:

$$ \gamma_0 = \beta_0, \qquad \gamma_1 = \frac{\beta_1}{c}, \qquad \gamma_2 = \beta_2 $$

Interpretation

If an explanatory variable is multiplied by \(c\), its slope is divided by \(c\). The intercept and the slopes of the other regressors do not change.

Start from the fitted equation \( \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_1 + \hat{\beta}_2 x_2 \).

$$ \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \frac{\hat{\beta}_1}{c}(cx_1) + \hat{\beta}_2 x_2 $$

Defining \(x_1^* = cx_1\):

$$ \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \frac{\hat{\beta}_1}{c}\,x_1^* + \hat{\beta}_2 x_2 $$

So the new coefficient is \( \hat{\gamma}_1 = \hat{\beta}_1 / c \).

Effect on the Standard Error

$$ se(\hat{\gamma}_1) = \frac{se(\hat{\beta}_1)}{|c|} $$

Recall the general formula:

$$ se(\hat{\beta}_j) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{SST_j(1 - R_j^2)}} $$

For \(x_1^* = cx_1\), we have \(SST_{x_1^*} = c^2 SST_{x_1}\). Plugging in:

$$ se(\hat{\gamma}_1) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{c^2 SST_{x_1}(1 - R_1^2)}} = \frac{1}{|c|} \cdot \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{SST_{x_1}(1 - R_1^2)}} = \frac{se(\hat{\beta}_1)}{|c|} $$

t-statistic and p-value

Because both the estimate and its standard error are divided by the same factor:

$$ t_{\hat{\gamma}_1} = \frac{\hat{\gamma}_1}{se(\hat{\gamma}_1)} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_1/c}{se(\hat{\beta}_1)/c} = t_{\hat{\beta}_1} $$
  • t-statistic unchanged
  • p-value unchanged
  • statistical significance unchanged

Exam Reflex

Scale an \(x_j\) variable \(\Rightarrow\) only its own slope and standard error change.

Scaling a Dependent Variable

Now suppose the dependent variable is multiplied by \(c\). Multiply both sides of the original model by \(c\):

$$ cy = c\beta_0 + c\beta_1 x_1 + c\beta_2 x_2 + cu $$

So in the rescaled model:

$$ \gamma_j = c\beta_j \qquad \text{for all } j = 0,1,2 $$

If the dependent variable is multiplied by \(c\), all coefficients are multiplied by \(c\).

Start from \( \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_1 + \hat{\beta}_2 x_2 \). Multiply by \(c\):

$$ c\hat{y} = c\hat{\beta}_0 + c\hat{\beta}_1 x_1 + c\hat{\beta}_2 x_2 $$

Thus every coefficient is multiplied by \(c\): \(\hat{\gamma}_j = c\hat{\beta}_j\).

Effect on the Standard Error

$$ se(\hat{\gamma}_j) = |c| \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

In the rescaled model \(y^* = cy\), the new residuals are \(\hat{u}^* = c\hat{u}\), so:

$$ \hat{\sigma}^* = |c|\,\hat{\sigma} $$

But the regressors \(x_1, x_2\) are unchanged, so \(SST_j\) and \(R_j^2\) stay the same. Therefore:

$$ se(\hat{\gamma}_j) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}^*}{\sqrt{SST_j(1-R_j^2)}} = \frac{|c|\,\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{SST_j(1-R_j^2)}} = |c| \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

t-statistic and p-value

Because both the estimate and its standard error are multiplied by the same factor:

$$ t_{\hat{\gamma}_j} = \frac{c\hat{\beta}_j}{|c| \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j)} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} = t_{\hat{\beta}_j} $$
  • t-statistic unchanged
  • p-value unchanged
  • statistical significance unchanged

Case Affected Unchanged Rule
Scale independent variable own slope, own SE t-stat, p-value, other slopes divide slope by \(c\)
Scale dependent variable all coefficients, all SEs t-stat, p-value multiply all coefficients by \(c\)

Scaling in Logarithmic Models

Logarithmic models behave differently from level models. When scaling happens inside a logarithm, the change is absorbed by the intercept.

Case 1 — Dependent variable in logs

$$ \log(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

If \(y\) is rescaled by \(c\), the new dependent variable is \(\log(cy)\). Using the log property:

$$ \log(cy) = \log(c) + \log(y) $$

Therefore:

$$ \gamma_0 = \beta_0 + \log(c), \qquad \gamma_1 = \beta_1, \qquad \gamma_2 = \beta_2 $$

Start from the original model:

$$ \log(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Replace \(\log(y)\) with \(\log(cy) - \log(c)\):

$$ \log(cy) - \log(c) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Move \(\log(c)\) to the right-hand side:

$$ \log(cy) = (\beta_0 + \log(c)) + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

The new intercept is \(\gamma_0 = \beta_0 + \log(c)\). All slopes remain the same.

Case 2 — Independent variable in logs

$$ \log(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \log(x_1) + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

If \(x_1\) is rescaled by \(c\), we get \(\log(cx_1) = \log(c) + \log(x_1)\). Therefore:

$$ \gamma_0 = \beta_0 + \beta_1\log(c), \qquad \gamma_1 = \beta_1, \qquad \gamma_2 = \beta_2 $$

Substitute \(\log(cx_1) = \log(c) + \log(x_1)\) into the model:

$$ \log(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1[\log(c) + \log(x_1)] + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

Expand:

$$ \log(y) = (\beta_0 + \beta_1\log(c)) + \beta_1\log(x_1) + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$

The new intercept is \(\gamma_0 = \beta_0 + \beta_1\log(c)\). Note that the constant \(\beta_1\log(c)\) appears because \(\log(c)\) is multiplied by the slope \(\beta_1\) of the logged variable. All slopes remain the same.

Key Logic

Inside logs, scaling becomes a constant shift. That shift goes into the intercept, not into the slopes.

Quadratic Models

Quadratic models are used when the effect of a regressor is not constant. Consider:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \beta_2 x^2 + u $$

The marginal effect of \(x\) on \(y\) is:

$$ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = \beta_1 + 2\beta_2 x $$

So the effect of \(x\) depends on the level of \(x\).

Turning Point

Set the marginal effect equal to zero:

$$ \beta_1 + 2\beta_2 x = 0 \implies x^* = -\frac{\beta_1}{2\beta_2} $$
  • if \(\beta_2 < 0\), the function is concave → turning point is a maximum
  • if \(\beta_2 > 0\), the function is convex → turning point is a minimum

Exam Reflex

In a quadratic model, do not interpret \(\beta_1\) alone as the effect of \(x\).

Quadratics with Logarithms

We can combine nonlinearities and logarithms:

$$ \log(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \log(x) + \beta_2[\log(x)]^2 + u $$

Important: the square is on \(\log(x)\), not inside the logarithm. The elasticity is now:

$$ \frac{\partial\log(y)}{\partial\log(x)} = \beta_1 + 2\beta_2\log(x) $$

So the percentage effect of \(x\) on \(y\) depends on the level of \(x\).

Interpretation

This model allows elasticity itself to vary across observations.

Interaction Terms

Interaction terms are used when the effect of one explanatory variable depends on another. Consider:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_3 x_1 x_2 + u $$

The marginal effects are:

$$ \frac{\partial y}{\partial x_1} = \beta_1 + \beta_3 x_2 \qquad \frac{\partial y}{\partial x_2} = \beta_2 + \beta_3 x_1 $$

Testing for Interaction

To test whether the interaction effect exists, we test:

$$ H_0: \beta_3 = 0 \qquad H_1: \beta_3 \neq 0 $$

If we reject \(H_0\), the effect of \(x_1\) on \(y\) genuinely depends on the level of \(x_2\).

Important Warning

In an interaction model, \(\beta_1\) is not the universal effect of \(x_1\). It is the effect only when \(x_2 = 0\).

Goodness-of-Fit Measures

The coefficient of determination is:

$$ R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST} $$

It measures the fraction of the total variation in \(y\) explained by the model.

Important Limitation

Adding a new regressor never decreases \(R^2\). That is why ordinary \(R^2\) is not a reliable tool for variable selection.

Adjusted \(R^2\)

To penalize unnecessary complexity, we use the adjusted coefficient of determination:

$$ \bar{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{SSR/(n-k-1)}{SST/(n-1)} $$

This can be rewritten as:

$$ \bar{R}^2 = 1 - (1-R^2)\frac{n-1}{n-k-1} $$

Start from \( \bar{R}^2 = 1 - \dfrac{SSR/(n-k-1)}{SST/(n-1)} \). Simplify:

$$ \bar{R}^2 = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST}\cdot\frac{n-1}{n-k-1} $$

From \(R^2 = 1 - SSR/SST\) we get \(SSR/SST = 1-R^2\). Substitute:

$$ \bar{R}^2 = 1 - (1-R^2)\frac{n-1}{n-k-1} $$

Why \(n-1\) and \(n-k-1\)?

  • \(n-1\) is the degrees of freedom of \(SST\), because one degree of freedom is lost when estimating \(\bar{y}\)
  • \(n-k-1\) is the degrees of freedom of \(SSR\), because we estimate \(k+1\) parameters: one intercept and \(k\) slopes

Adjusted \(R^2\) can be negative if the model fits very poorly. This simply means the model performs worse than a naive benchmark after the degrees-of-freedom correction.

Deep Logic

Adjusted \(R^2\) balances fit improvement against model complexity.

Two Key Properties

  • When one variable is added, adjusted \(R^2\) increases if \(|t| > 1\)
  • When a group of variables is added, adjusted \(R^2\) increases if \(F > 1\)

Non-Nested Model Comparison

Consider two models:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$ $$ y = \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 x_1 + \gamma_2 x_3 + v $$

These models are non-nested because neither one is a special case of the other. The standard F-test compares a restricted model to an unrestricted model — that logic only works when one model is contained inside the other. For non-nested models, the usual F-test is not valid.

How to compare

  • \(R^2\): only valid if both models have the same number of regressors and the same dependent variable form
  • \(\bar{R}^2\): preferred for comparing specifications, but the dependent variable must be in the same form (do not compare \(y\) vs \(\log(y)\))

Standardized Beta Coefficients

Sometimes variables are measured in very different units. To compare their relative importance, we standardize them. The standardized beta coefficient is:

$$ \hat{b}_j = \hat{\beta}_j \frac{\hat{\sigma}_j}{\hat{\sigma}_y} $$

where \(\hat{\sigma}_j\) is the sample standard deviation of \(x_j\) and \(\hat{\sigma}_y\) is the sample standard deviation of \(y\).

Interpretation: \(\hat{b}_j\) measures the change in \(y\), in standard deviations, caused by a one-standard-deviation increase in \(x_j\).

Start from the estimated model, subtract sample means:

$$ y_i - \bar{y} = \hat{\beta}_1(x_{1i} - \bar{x}_1) + \cdots + \hat{u}_i $$

Divide both sides by \(\hat{\sigma}_y\):

$$ \frac{y_i - \bar{y}}{\hat{\sigma}_y} = \hat{\beta}_1 \frac{\hat{\sigma}_1}{\hat{\sigma}_y} \cdot \frac{x_{1i} - \bar{x}_1}{\hat{\sigma}_1} + \cdots $$

Thus the standardized coefficient is \( \hat{b}_j = \hat{\beta}_j \hat{\sigma}_j / \hat{\sigma}_y \).

Important Note

Standardization changes interpretation, but not statistical significance.
