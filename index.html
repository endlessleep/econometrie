1.1 Simple Regression Model

Simple linear regression

The simple linear regression model describes the relationship between a dependent variable \( y \) and an independent variable \( x \).

Variables \( x \) and \( y \) are randomly sampled and stored in a dataset.

Important Assumption: Parameters \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\)

Parameters \( \beta_0 \) and \( \beta_1 \) represent and describe the relationship between \( x \) and \( y \). They are unknown and need to be estimated from the data.

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u $$
  • \( y \) : Dependent variable
  • \( x \) : Independent variable
  • \( \beta_0 \) : Intercept parameter - measures how \( y \) varies when \( x = 0 \).
  • \( \beta_1 \) : Slope parameter - measures 'how \( y \) varies with changes in \( x \)'.
  • \( u \) : Error term (or disturbance)
Interpretation of the slope :
\( \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x \)

Error term (\(u\))

The error term or disturbance \( u \) represents unobserved effects that influence \( y \) but are not included in the regression equation.

It captures, for example:

  • Omitted variables
  • Random shocks
  • Measurement errors
  • Unobservable factors

Logarithmic functional forms

Some relationships are not linear by their nature. For interpretational purposes, it is sometimes more appropriate or desirable to comment on the percentage effects. Logarithmic transformations are popular from the perspective of economics as they allow for elasticity and semi-elasticity interpretations.

Types of logarithmic models

Model Dependent variable Independent variable Interpretation of \( \beta_1 \)
Level-level \( y \) \( x \) \( \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x \)
Level-log \( y \) \( \log(x) \) \( \Delta y = (\beta_1/100)\%\Delta x \)
Log-level \( \log(y) \) \( x \) \( \%\Delta y = (100\beta_1)\Delta x \)
Log-log \( \log(y) \) \( \log(x) \) \( \%\Delta y = \beta_1 \%\Delta x \)

Expected value & Mean Independence

Expected value of the error term

As long as there is the intercept \( \beta_0 \) in the model, we can assume that:

$$ E(u) = 0 $$

This is rather a statement than an assumption, as we can always make it hold.

Mean independence & Zero Conditional Mean Assumption

Even though correlation might be appealing, it does not control for a large enough set of possible relationships (e.g., \( x \) vs. \( x^2 \) vs. \( |x| \)). It is much more appropriate to use conditional expectations.

Specifically, we assume that \( E(u|x) = E(u) \), i.e., \( u \) is mean independent of \( x \).

Combining this with \( E(u) = 0 \), we obtain the Zero conditional mean assumption:

$$ E(u|x) = 0 $$
$$ E(y|x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x $$

This equation is called the Population Regression Function (PRF).

  • Mind the difference between \( E(y|x) \) and \( y \) here.

Estimation Methods

Implied equations for parameter estimation

This leads to two practically important (implied) equations and motivations that we can use for the estimation of parameters \( \beta_0 \) and \( \beta_1 \):

  • \( E(u) = 0 \)
  • \( Cov(x,u) = E(xu) - E(x)E(u) = E(xu) = 0 \)

Method of Moments estimation

Method of moments (MM) estimation stems in specifying moments' restrictions and substituting them with their sample counterparts based on \( n \) observations in the dataset.

Theoretical expectations Sample moment conditions
\( E(u) = E(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x) = 0 \) \( \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \)
\( Cov(x,u) = E[x(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x)] = 0 \) \( \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \)

Derivation of \( \hat{\beta}_0 \)

From the first equation, we get:

$$ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \frac{1}{n} \sum y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 \frac{1}{n} \sum 1 - \hat{\beta}_1 \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i = 0 \iff $$ $$ \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Derivation of \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)

From the second equation, we get (substitute for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \) and solve for \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)):

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - (\bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}) - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) - \hat{\beta}_1 \sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x}) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x})} $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Primary formulation of the OLS Slope Estimator

As a byproduct, we also obtain the intercept estimator: \( \hat{\beta}_0^{OLS} = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \bar{x} \)

Alternative Notations of \( \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x})} $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum y_i (x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Least Squares Estimation

Estimated model computationally

Finally, for a given sample of data, we can express the estimated model numerically:

  • Sample Regression Function (SRF) or OLS regression line:
    \( \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x \)
  • Fitted value (for \( y \) when \( x = x_i \)):
    \( \hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_i \)
  • Residual:
    \( \hat{u}_i = y_i - \hat{y}_i = y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i \)
$$ SSR \equiv \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{u}_i^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i)^2 $$

Sum of Squared Residuals (SSR).
Specifying the SSR as a penalty function of interest, and looking for its minimum (First Order Conditions):

Partial derivatives (FOCs):

\( \frac{\partial SSR}{\partial \hat{\beta}_0} = 0 \implies -2 \sum (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \)

\( \frac{\partial SSR}{\partial \hat{\beta}_1} = 0 \implies -2 \sum x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \)

These are exactly comparable to the Method of Moments derivation, i.e., they lead to the same solution. Hence the name OLS: 'Ordinary Least Squares' estimator.
