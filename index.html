1.1 Simple Regression Model

Defining the Model

For variables \( x \) and \( y \), the simple linear regression model is defined as:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u $$

Variables \( x \) and \( y \) are randomly sampled and stored in a dataset.

  • \( y \) : Dependent variable
  • \( x \) : Independent variable
  • \( \beta_0 \) : Intercept parameter - measures how \( y \) varies when \( x = 0 \).
  • \( \beta_1 \) : Slope parameter - measures how \( y \) varies with changes in \( x \).
  • \( u \) : Error term or disturbance - represents unobserved effects that are not included in the regression equation.

Logarithmic Functional Forms

Some relationships are not linear by their nature. Logarithmic transformations allow for elasticity and semi-elasticity interpretations:

Model Dependent (y) Independent (x) Interpretation of \( \beta_1 \)
Level-level \( y \) \( x \) \( \Delta y = \beta_1 \Delta x \)
Level-log \( y \) \( \log(x) \) \( \Delta y = (\beta_1 / 100) \% \Delta x \)
Log-level \( \log(y) \) \( x \) \( \% \Delta y = (100 \cdot \beta_1) \Delta x \)
Log-log \( \log(y) \) \( \log(x) \) \( \% \Delta y = \beta_1 \% \Delta x \)

Expected Value & Mean Independence

As long as there is the intercept \( \beta_0 \) in the model, we can assume that: \( E(u) = 0 \).

Zero Conditional Mean Assumption

We assume that \( E(u|x) = E(u) \), meaning \( u \) is mean independent of \( x \). Combining this with \( E(u) = 0 \), we obtain:

$$ E(u|x) = 0 $$

This leads to the Population Regression Function (PRF):

$$ E(y|x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x $$

Method of Moments Estimation

This leads to two practically important implied equations used for the estimation of parameters \( \beta_0 \) and \( \beta_1 \):

  • Theoretical: \( E(u) = E(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x) = 0 \)
  • Theoretical: \( Cov(x,u) = E[x(y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x)] = 0 \)

Estimator for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \)

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

From the first equation, we substitute sample moments for theoretical moments and solve for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \):

$$ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \frac{1}{n} \sum y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 \frac{1}{n} \sum 1 - \hat{\beta}_1 \frac{1}{n} \sum x_i = 0 \iff $$ $$ \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Estimator for \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x})} $$

From the second equation, we get (substitute for \( \hat{\beta}_0 \) and solve for \(\hat{\beta}_1 \)):

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i (y_i - \hat{\beta}_0 - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - (\bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x}) - \hat{\beta}_1 x_i) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) - \hat{\beta}_1 \sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x}) = 0 \iff $$ $$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x})} $$

Alternative Notations of the OLS Slope Estimator \( \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \)

The OLS estimator is perfectly equivalent to the MM estimator above. Here are alternative ways \( \hat{\beta}_1^{OLS} \) can be written:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Knowing that \( \sum(x_i - \bar{x})\bar{y} = \bar{y} \sum(x_i - \bar{x}) = \bar{y} \cdot 0 = 0 \), we can factorize the numerator \( \sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y}) \):

$$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) = \sum (x_i - \bar{x} + \bar{x}) (y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + \sum \bar{x}(y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + \bar{x}\sum(y_i - \bar{y}) $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) + 0 $$ $$ = \sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y}) $$

And knowing that \( \sum x_i \bar{x} = \bar{x} \sum x_i = n\bar{x}^2 = \sum \bar{x}^2 \), we can rewrite the denominator \( \sum x_i(x_i - \bar{x}) \):

$$ \sum x_i (x_i - \bar{x}) = \sum x_i^2 - \sum x_i \bar{x} $$ $$ = \sum x_i^2 - n\bar{x}^2 = \sum x_i^2 - 2n\bar{x}^2 + n\bar{x}^2 $$ $$ = \sum x_i^2 - 2\bar{x}\sum x_i + \sum \bar{x}^2 $$ $$ = \sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum y_i (x_i - \bar{x})}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

To go from \( \sum x_i(y_i - \bar{y}) \) to \( \sum y_i(x_i - \bar{x}) \), we simply expand and refactor:

$$ \sum x_i (y_i - \bar{y}) = \sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \bar{y} $$

Since \( \sum x_i \bar{y} = n\bar{x}\bar{y} = \sum \bar{x} y_i \):

$$ \sum x_i y_i - \sum \bar{x} y_i = \sum y_i (x_i - \bar{x}) $$

1.2 OLS Properties

Algebraic Properties of OLS

1. Sum of residuals equals zero

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

The sum (or sample mean) of the OLS residuals is zero.

This follows from the first first-order condition (FOC) of the OLS minimization problem.

2. Residuals are orthogonal to the explanatory variable

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

The sample covariance between the explanatory variable \( x \) and the residuals is zero.

This follows from the second first-order condition (FOC) of the OLS minimization problem.

3. Observed values can be decomposed

Each observed value of \( y \) can be decomposed into a fitted value and a residual:

$$ y_i = \hat{y}_i + \hat{u}_i $$

The fitted values and residuals are orthogonal:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{y}_i \hat{u}_i = 0 $$

Since \( \hat{y}_i = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x_i \), it is a linear combination of constants and \( x \), both of which are orthogonal to the residuals.

4. Means of observed and fitted values are equal

$$ \bar{y} = \overline{\hat{y}} $$

Equivalently: \( \sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{y}_i \)

Since \( y_i = \hat{y}_i + \hat{u}_i \), summing both sides gives \( \sum y_i = \sum \hat{y}_i + \sum \hat{u}_i \). Because \( \sum \hat{u}_i = 0 \), we get \( \sum y_i = \sum \hat{y}_i \).

5. The point (\( \bar{x},\bar{y} \)) lies on the OLS regression line

Regression line: \( \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 x \)

If \( x = \bar{x} \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} = \bar{y} $$

Therefore the point \( (\bar{x},\bar{y}) \) always lies on the regression line.

We know that \( \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} \). Rearranging this formula directly yields \( \bar{y} = \hat{\beta}_0 + \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} \).

Sums of Squares

Term Name Formula Measures...
SST Total Sum of Squares \( \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2 \) Total variation of \( y \) around its mean.
SSE Explained Sum of Squares \( \sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})^2 \) Variation explained by the regression model.
SSR Residual Sum of Squares \( \sum \hat{u}_i^2 \) Unexplained variation.

Decomposition of variance

$$ SST = SSE + SSR $$

Total variation = explained variation + unexplained variation.

Write \( y_i - \bar{y} = (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y}) + \hat{u}_i \). Square both sides and sum to get SST. The cross product term \( 2\sum (\hat{y}_i - \bar{y})\hat{u}_i \) equals zero by the algebraic properties of OLS residuals discussed above.

Goodness of Fit

Coefficient of determination (\( R^2 \))

$$ R^2 = \frac{SSE}{SST} = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST} $$

Interpretation

\( R^2 \) measures the fraction of the sample variation in \( y \) explained by \( x \).

  • Range: \( 0 \leq R^2 \leq 1 \)
  • It is sometimes reported in percentages.
  • Important property: \( R^2 \) cannot decrease when adding an explanatory variable.
  • Threshold values depend on: the discipline, the field of economics, and the type of data.

Unbiasedness of OLS

The unbiasedness of the OLS estimator relies on the SLR (Simple Linear Regression) assumptions.

SLR.1 Linear in parameters
\( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + u \)
\( \beta_0 \) is the population intercept, \( \beta_1 \) is the population slope. The presence of the intercept implies \( E(u) = 0 \).
SLR.2 Random sampling
We observe a random sample of size \( n \) drawn from the population.
SLR.3 Variation in the explanatory variable
The sample values of \( x \) are not all identical: \( \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2 \neq 0 \).
SLR.4 Zero conditional mean
\( E(u|x) = 0 \)
This implies \( Cov(x,u) = 0 \) and \( E(u) = 0 \).

Unbiasedness result

Under assumptions SLR.1–SLR.4,

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 \quad \text{and} \quad E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 $$

Therefore, \( \hat{\beta}_0 \) and \( \hat{\beta}_1 \) are unbiased estimators.

Start from the OLS estimator:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Using the linear model:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i $$

Substitute:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i)(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Expand:

$$ = \beta_0 \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} + \beta_1 \frac{\sum x_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} + \frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Use properties:

$$ \sum (x_i-\bar{x}) = 0 $$ $$ \sum x_i(x_i-\bar{x}) = \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 $$

Thus:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Take expectations conditional on \( x \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})E(u_i)}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Using the assumption \( E(u_i|x_i)=0 \) we obtain:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1)=\beta_1 $$

Starting from the population model and the zero conditional mean assumption:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + u_i $$ $$ E(u_i \mid x_i) = 0 $$

Recall the OLS identity for the intercept:

$$ \hat{\beta}_0 = \bar{y} - \hat{\beta}_1 \bar{x} $$

Take expectations (conditionally on \( x \)):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = E(\bar{y}) - \bar{x}E(\hat{\beta}_1) $$

Now, compute \( E(\bar{y}) \):

$$ \bar{y} = \frac{1}{n}\sum y_i \implies E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum E(y_i) $$

From the population model, \( E(y_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i \). Substituting this:

$$ E(\bar{y}) = \frac{1}{n}\sum (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i) $$ $$ E(\bar{y}) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x} $$

Substitute this result back into the expectation of \( \hat{\beta}_0 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = (\beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x}) - \bar{x}E(\hat{\beta}_1) $$

Finally, using the unbiasedness of the slope \( E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \bar{x} - \beta_1 \bar{x} $$ $$ E(\hat{\beta}_0) = \beta_0 $$

Variance of the OLS Estimators

Homoskedasticity assumption

SLR.5 Homoskedasticity: The variance of the error term is constant:

$$ Var(u|x) = \sigma^2 $$

This means the variance of the errors does not depend on \( x \). Under this assumption: \( Var(y|x) = \sigma^2 \).

Opposite case: Heteroskedasticity.

Under SLR.1–SLR.5:

Variance of \( \hat{\beta}_1 \)

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Variance of \( \hat{\beta}_0 \)

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_0) = \frac{\sigma^2 \sum x_i^2}{n \sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} $$

Start from the rewritten OLS estimator:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Since the variance of a constant is zero:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1) = Var\left(\frac{\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}\right) $$

Factor out the constant denominator:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} Var\left(\sum u_i(x_i-\bar{x})\right) $$

Using the variance of a sum:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum Var\left(u_i(x_i-\bar{x})\right) $$

Since \( x_i \) are constants:

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 Var(u_i) $$

Using \( Var(u_i)=\sigma^2 \):

$$ = \frac{1}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} \sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2 \sigma^2 $$

Factor out \( \sigma^2 \):

$$ = \sigma^2 \frac{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{\left(\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2\right)^2} $$

Simplify:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}_1)=\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2} $$

Estimating the Error Variance

The variance of the error term \( \sigma^2 \) is not observed and must be estimated. The unbiased estimator is:

$$ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n-2}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \hat{u}_i^2 $$

We divide by \( n-2 \) because we lose two degrees of freedom. Two restrictions exist on residuals: \( \sum \hat{u}_i = 0 \) and \( \sum x_i \hat{u}_i = 0 \).

Standard errors

  • Standard error of the regression: \( \hat{\sigma} \).
  • Standard error of \( \hat{\beta}_1 \):
    $$ se(\hat{\beta}_1) = \frac{\hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \bar{x})^2}} $$
    This is used to construct test statistics and confidence intervals.

Regression through the Origin

In rare cases we impose: \( \beta_0 = 0 \). The model becomes:

$$ y = \beta_1 x + u $$

OLS estimator:

$$ \tilde{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i y_i}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2} $$

Important Warning

If in reality \( \beta_0 \neq 0 \), then \( \tilde{\beta}_1 \) is biased. Therefore regression through the origin is rarely used in practice.

Important note: The definition of \( R^2 \) changes for regressions through the origin.

1.3 Multiple Regression Model

Model with \( k \) Independent Variables

In multiple regression, we extend the simple model to include multiple explanatory variables:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \dots + \beta_k x_k + u $$

Key Assumption

$$ E(u \mid x_1, x_2, \dots, x_k) = 0 $$

Meaning: The error term is uncorrelated with all explanatory variables (Zero Conditional Mean). This ensures that estimators are unbiased.

Matrix Notation

We represent the model using matrices for cleaner derivations:

$$ Y = X\beta + u $$

Vector \( Y \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{pmatrix} $$
$$ = $$

Matrix \( X \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_{11} & \dots & x_{1k} \\ 1 & x_{21} & \dots & x_{2k} \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 1 & x_{n1} & \dots & x_{nk} \end{pmatrix} $$

Vector \( \beta \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_k \end{pmatrix} $$
$$ + $$

Vector \( u \)

$$ \begin{pmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_n \end{pmatrix} $$

Matrix Calculus Reference

Essential derivatives for MCO matrix proofs:

Expression Derivative w.r.t. \( \beta \)
\( a^T \beta \) or \( \beta^T a \) \( a \)
\( \beta^T \beta \) \( 2\beta \)
\( \beta^T A \beta \) (A symmetric) \( 2A\beta \)
\( \beta^T A \beta \) (A non-symmetric) \( (A + A^T)\beta \)
\( (A\beta)^T (A\beta) \) \( 2A^T A \beta \)

OLS Estimator (Matrix Form)

The vector of OLS estimates is given by the Normal Equations:

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y $$

The vector \(\hat{\beta}\) contains the \((k+1)\) estimated coefficients (intercept + \(k\) slopes).

Start with residuals: \( \hat{u} = Y - X\hat{\beta} \). Minimize the Sum of Squared Residuals (SSR):

$$ SSR = \hat{u}^T \hat{u} = (Y - X\hat{\beta})^T (Y - X\hat{\beta}) $$

Expand the quadratic form:

$$ SSR = Y^T Y - Y^T X\hat{\beta} - \hat{\beta}^T X^T Y + \hat{\beta}^T X^T X \hat{\beta} $$ $$ SSR = Y^T Y - 2\hat{\beta}^T X^T Y + \hat{\beta}^T X^T X \hat{\beta} $$

Take the derivative with respect to \( \hat{\beta} \) (F.O.C.):

$$ \frac{\partial SSR}{\partial \hat{\beta}} = -2X^T Y + 2(X^T X)\hat{\beta} = 0 $$

Solve for \( \hat{\beta} \):

$$ (X^T X)\hat{\beta} = X^T Y \implies \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y $$

Interpretation of Coefficients

Intercept (\( \hat{\beta}_0 \)): The predicted value of \( y \) when all independent variables are zero (\( x_1 = x_2 = \dots = x_k = 0 \)).

Slope Coefficients (\( \hat{\beta}_j \)): Measures the partial effect of \( x_j \) on \( y \).

$$ \Delta \hat{y} = \hat{\beta}_j \Delta x_j $$

This represents the change in \( y \) for one unit change in \( x_j \), holding all other variables fixed (ceteris paribus).

Multiple Linear Regression Assumptions

Gauss-Markov assumptions for unbiasedness in multiple regression:

MLR.1 Linear in Parameters
\( y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \dots + \beta_k x_k + u \).
MLR.2 Random Sampling
We observe a random sample reflecting the population.
MLR.3 No Perfect Collinearity
No independent variable is a perfect linear combination of others. \( X \) has full column rank.
MLR.4 Zero Conditional Mean
\( E(u \mid x_1, \dots, x_k) = 0 \). This implies \( Cov(x_j, u) = 0 \).

Unbiasedness of OLS

Under assumptions MLR.1 through MLR.4, the OLS estimator is unbiased:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}) = \beta $$

This means \( E(\hat{\beta}_j) = \beta_j \) for all \( j = 0, \dots, k \).

Start with the OLS estimator and substitute \( Y = X\beta + u \):

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T Y = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T (X\beta + u) $$

Distribute the terms:

$$ \hat{\beta} = (X^T X)^{-1} (X^T X) \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u $$ $$ \hat{\beta} = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u $$

Take expectations conditional on \( X \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T E(u \mid X) $$

Using MLR.4 \( E(u \mid X) = 0 \):

$$ E(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = \beta + 0 = \beta $$

Model Misspecification

Analyzing the impact of model choice on estimator properties.

Overspecified Model

Adding irrelevant variables (where true \(\beta_j = 0\)).

Result: MCO remains unbiased, but variance may increase.

Underspecified Model

Omitting a relevant variable (Omitted Variable Bias).

Result: MCO estimators become biased if \( Corr(x_1, x_{omitted}) \neq 0 \).

Bias in misspecified models: The true population model is:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + u_i $$

But we estimate a simple regression with only one explanatory variable:

$$ y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + u_i $$

The OLS estimator is:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} y_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Substitute the true model for \( y_i \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (\beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + u_i) (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$ $$ = \frac{ \beta_0 \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \beta_1 \sum x_{i1}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \beta_2 \sum x_{i2}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) + \sum u_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) }{\sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Using properties of sample means: \( \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) = 0 \) and \( \sum x_{i1}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1) = \sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2 \):

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_{i2}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Taking expectations:

$$ E(\hat{\beta}_1) = \beta_1 + \beta_2 \frac{\sum (x_{i2}-\bar{x}_2)(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)}{\sum (x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2} $$

Summary of Omitted Variable Bias

Relation \( \beta_2 > 0 \) \( \beta_2 < 0 \)
\( Corr(x_1, x_2) > 0 \) Positive Bias Negative Bias
\( Corr(x_1, x_2) < 0 \) Negative Bias Positive Bias

Variance of OLS Estimators

To derive the variance, we add the final Gauss-Markov assumption:

MLR.5 Homoskedasticity

$$ Var(u \mid X) = \sigma^2 I_n $$

The variance of residuals is constant across all observations.

Under MLR.1–MLR.5, the variance-covariance matrix of \( \hat{\beta} \) is:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta}) = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} $$

Start with the linear expression: \( \hat{\beta} = \beta + (X^T X)^{-1} X^T u \).

The variance is given by:

$$ Var(\hat{\beta} \mid X) = Var((X^T X)^{-1} X^T u \mid X) $$

Using the property \( Var(Az) = A Var(z) A^T \):

$$ = [(X^T X)^{-1} X^T] Var(u \mid X) [(X^T X)^{-1} X^T]^T $$ $$ = (X^T X)^{-1} X^T (\sigma^2 I_n) X (X^T X)^{-1} $$ $$ = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} (X^T X) (X^T X)^{-1} $$ $$ = \sigma^2 (X^T X)^{-1} $$

Estimating Variance & Standard Errors

The unbiased estimator for \( \sigma^2 \) in multiple regression is:

$$ \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{SSR}{n - k - 1} $$

Degrees of Freedom (df): \( n - (k+1) \). We subtract the number of estimated parameters.

Standard Errors of individual coefficients:

$$ se(\hat{\beta}_j) = \hat{\sigma} \sqrt{ [(X^T X)^{-1}]_{jj} } $$

Alternatively: \( Var(\hat{\beta}_j) = \frac{\sigma^2}{SST_j(1 - R_j^2)} \), where \( R_j^2 \) is the R-squared from regressing \( x_j \) on all other independent variables.

1.4 Multiple Regression Analysis: Inference

Gauss-Markov Theorem

Under assumptions MLR.1 through MLR.5 (Gauss-Markov assumptions), the OLS estimator is the BLUE:

$$ \text{B.L.U.E. = Best Linear Unbiased Estimator} $$
  • Linear: \(\hat{\beta}_j\) can be expressed as a linear function of \(y_i\): \(\hat{\beta}_j = \sum w_{ij} y_i \).
  • Best: Within the class of linear unbiased estimators, OLS has the minimum variance (it is efficient).

For simple regression, the OLS estimator for the slope is:

$$ \hat{\beta}_1 = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x}) y_i}{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2} = \sum w_i y_i $$

where the weights are \( w_i = \frac{x_i - \bar{x}}{\sum (x_k - \bar{x})^2} \). Since \(w_i\) only depends on \(x\), \(\hat{\beta}_1\) is a linear function of \(y\).

Normality Assumption (MLR.6)

To perform statistical inference (tests and confidence intervals), we need to know the distribution of the error term:

MLR.6 Normality

$$ u \sim N(0, \sigma^2) $$

The error \(u\) is independent of \(x\) and follows a Normal Distribution.

Classical Linear Model (CLM): Assumptions MLR.1 to MLR.6 together are called the CLM assumptions.

Under CLM, the OLS estimator is BUE (Best Unbiased Estimator), which is even stronger than BLUE.

Normal Sampling Distribution

Under MLR.1–MLR.6, the OLS estimators follow a normal distribution:

$$ \hat{\beta}_j \sim N\left( \beta_j, Var(\hat{\beta}_j) \right) $$

Standardizing gives us the standard normal result (if \(\sigma\) was known):

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{sd(\hat{\beta}_j)} \sim N(0, 1) $$

However, in practice we must estimate \(\sigma\), leading us to the \(t\)-distribution.

Hypothesis Testing (t-test)

Under CLM assumptions, the standardized estimator follows a \(t\)-distribution:

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} \sim t_{n-k-1} $$

t-ratio for \(H_0: \beta_j = 0\):

$$ t_{\hat{\beta}_j} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

We reject \(H_0\) if \(|t| > t_{critical}\) or if \(p\text{-value} < \alpha\).

One-sided Test

\(H_1: \beta_j > 0\) (or \(< 0\))
Focus on one tail.

Two-sided Test

\(H_1: \beta_j \neq 0\)
Focus on both tails.

Confidence Intervals

A \((1-\alpha)\) confidence interval for \(\beta_j\) is constructed as:

$$ \hat{\beta}_j \pm t_{n-k-1, 1-\alpha/2} \cdot se(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

Rule of thumb (95% CI): For large samples (\(df > 120\)), the critical value is approx. \(2\).

If the CI contains \(0\), we cannot reject the null hypothesis \(H_0: \beta_j = 0\) at the \(\alpha\) significance level.

Economic vs. Statistical Significance

Statistical Significance

Is the \(p\)-value small enough? Is the effect different from zero purely due to chance?

Economic Significance

Is the magnitude of \(\beta_j\) large enough to matter in the real world?

Caution: A variable can be statistically significant but economically irrelevant (very small effect), or vice-versa (large effect but high standard error).

Testing Linear Combinations

Sometimes we want to test if two parameters are equal: \(H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2\).

The t-statistic:

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_2}{se(\hat{\beta}_1 - \hat{\beta}_2)} $$

Define \(\theta = \beta_1 - \beta_2\). The null is \(H_0: \theta = 0\).

Substitute \(\beta_1 = \theta + \beta_2\) into the original model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + (\theta + \beta_2)x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + u $$ $$ y = \beta_0 + \theta x_1 + \beta_2(x_1 + x_2) + u $$

By defining a new variable \(x^* = x_1 + x_2\), we can estimate the model and the \(t\)-ratio for \(x_1\) will directly test our hypothesis.

🎯 t-test (one-sided, right) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j \le \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j > \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } t > c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(t > c \mid H_0) = \alpha $$ $$ P(t < c) = 1 - \alpha $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( t_{obs} \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 t-test (one-sided, left) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j \ge \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j < \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } t < c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;\alpha} = -t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(t < c \mid H_0) = \alpha $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;\alpha} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( t_{obs} \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 t-test (two-sided) — Econometrics

🔹 1. Hypotheses

$$ H_0 : \beta_j = \beta_{j,0} \quad \text{vs} \quad H_1 : \beta_j \neq \beta_{j,0} $$

🔹 2. Test statistic

$$ t = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$
$$ \text{Under } H_0 : \quad t \sim t_{n - k - 1} $$

🔹 3. Decision rule

$$ \text{Reject } H_0 \text{ if } |t| > c $$

🔹 4. Critical value (table)

$$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation.

$$ P(-c < t < c \mid H_0) = 1 - \alpha $$ $$ P(t < -c \ \text{or} \ t > c) = \alpha $$ $$ P(t < -c) = P(t > c) = \frac{\alpha}{2} $$ $$ P(t < c) = 1 - \frac{\alpha}{2} $$ $$ c = t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} $$

🔹 5. Observed value (coef / SE)

$$ t_{obs} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_{j,0}}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} $$

🔹 6. Conclusion

Compare \( |t_{obs}| \) with \( c \) and conclude whether to reject \( H_0 \) or not.

🎯 Confidence Interval

$$ \hat{\beta}_j - t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) \;\le\; \beta_j \;\le\; \hat{\beta}_j + t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_j \;\mp\; t_{n-k-1,\;1-\alpha/2} \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

Note: if \(df > 120\), it is fine to use the standard normal approximation for the critical value \(t\).

To verify, we can also compare \(\beta_{j,0}\) to the confidence interval: if it lies within the interval, this confirms that we do not reject \(H_0\).

🔍 \(-c < t < c\)

$$ -c < \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{SE(\hat{\beta}_j)} < c $$
$$ -c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) < \hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j < c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$
$$ \hat{\beta}_j - c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) < \beta_j < \hat{\beta}_j + c \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_j) $$

\(Corr(x_2, x_1) > 0\) \(Corr(x_2, x_1) < 0\)

PART 2: Theoretical Derivations & OLS Assumptions

A survival guide to what each assumption does, separating small-sample from large-sample properties.

Assumption Meaning
(S)MLR.1 Linear in parameters (\(\beta\)).
(S)MLR.2 Random sampling from the population.
(S)MLR.3 No perfect collinearity / sample variation in \(x\). Allows matrix invertibility.
(S)MLR.4 Zero conditional mean: \(E(u|x) = 0\).
Crucial for UNBIASEDNESS. Weak form: \(Cov(x,u)=0\) (Crucial for CONSISTENCY).
(S)MLR.5 Homoskedasticity: \(Var(u|x) = \sigma^2\).
Required for EFFICIENCY (BLUE) and valid standard errors.
MLR.6 Normality of residuals: \(u \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\).
Required ONLY for EXACT \(t\) and \(F\) tests in SMALL samples.

Deep Dive Exams Explanations: Unbiasedness vs Consistency vs BLUE

1. Unbiasedness (Small Sample Property)

Requires: MLR.1 through MLR.4 (\(E(u|x) = 0\))

Unbiasedness means that if we draw many samples and run regressions on all of them, the average of all our \(\hat{\beta}\) estimates will exactly equal the true population \(\beta\). Crucially: we DO NOT need MLR.5 (homoskedasticity) or MLR.6 (normality) to be unbiased. As long as MLR.4 holds (the unobserved errors \(u\) don't systematically change with our \(x\) variables), OLS is unbiased.

2. Consistency (\(\text{plim}\), Large Sample Asymptotics - Lecture 1.5)

Requires: MLR.1 through MLR.4' (\(Cov(x,u) = 0\))

Consistency is a large sample property (asymptotics, \(n \to \infty\)). The \(\text{plim}\) (probability limit) of \(\hat{\beta}\) converges exactly to the true \(\beta\).

Why is it different from Unbiasedness? For consistency, we can actually relax MLR.4. We do NOT need the strict Zero Conditional Mean (\(E(u|x)=0\)). Instead, we only need the weaker condition of Zero Correlation (\(Cov(x,u)=0\)).

Exam Trap: "Do we need Homoskedasticity (MLR.5) or Normality (MLR.6) for consistency?" NO! Absolutely not. Even if variance explodes (heteroskedasticity) or residuals are not normally distributed, as long as \(x\) and \(u\) are uncorrelated, our estimator will consistently hit the true target as sample size grows.

3. Efficiency & Gauss-Markov (BLUE)

Requires: MLR.1 through MLR.5

Gauss-Markov states that OLS is the "Best Linear Unbiased Estimator" (BLUE). "Best" strictly means "Lowest Variance".

If MLR.5 (Homoskedasticity) fails (meaning variance of errors changes depending on \(x\)), OLS is Still Unbiased and Consistent, but it is No Longer Efficient (Not BLUE). The standard errors will be incorrectly calculated, causing \(t\)-tests to be wrong, but the estimate \(\hat{\beta}\) itself is still valid.

4. Role of Normality (MLR.6) vs. Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

In Small Samples: We desperately need MLR.6 (\(u\) is normally distributed). Without it, the \(\hat{\beta}\) distribution is unknown, and we mathematically cannot use Student's \(t\)-test or \(F\)-test critical values.

In Large Samples (Asymptotics): This is the magic of Lecture 1.5! Because the error term \(u\) is the sum of many unobserved factors, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) guarantees that \(\hat{\beta}\) will have an approximate normal distribution regardless of the underlying distribution of \(u\).
\(\Rightarrow\) Exam conclusion: In large samples, we can safely drop MLR.6. Standard \(t\) and \(F\) tests are perfectly valid asymptotically just by using CLT.

1.5 Testing Multiple Linear Restrictions & OLS Asymptotics

🎯 TL;DR: What to know for the Exam

  • 👉 F-test: Know the \(SSR\) vs \(R^2\) formulas by heart! Often asked to compute \(F\) from output variables.
  • 👉 Consistency (\(\text{plim}\)): You DO NOT need MLR.5 (homoskedasticity) or MLR.6 (normality) for consistency, only zero correlation.
  • 👉 Normality: Large sample + CLT \(\Rightarrow\) we no longer need the restrictive MLR.6 assumption for standard \(t\) and \(F\) tests.

Testing Exclusion Restrictions

We are interested in testing multiple hypotheses jointly (e.g., \(H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0\)). The \(t\)-statistic is not appropriate here, as we need to test the restrictions jointly.

Unrestricted vs. Restricted Models

Unrestricted Model:

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \beta_3 x_3 + u $$

Restricted Model (assuming \(H_0\)):

$$ y = \beta_0 + \beta_3 x_3 + u $$
Key concept: \(SSR\) of the unrestricted model (\(SSR_U\)) will always be lower (or equal) to the \(SSR\) of the restricted model (\(SSR_R\)).

The \(F\) Test

To judge whether the restricted model performs significantly worse, we use the \(F\) statistic:

$$ F = \frac{(SSR_R - SSR_U)/q}{SSR_U/(n-k-1)} \;\sim\; F_{q, \;n-k-1} $$
  • \(q\): number of restrictions (numerator \(df\))
  • \(n-k-1\): degrees of freedom of the unrestricted model (denominator \(df\))
  • \(F \ge 0\) always!

\(SSR\) vs \(R^2\) form of the statistic

Using the definition \(R^2 = 1 - \frac{SSR}{SST}\), we can substitute \(SSR = (1-R^2)SST\).

$$ F = \frac{(1 - R_R^2)SST - (1 - R_U^2)SST}{q} \div \frac{(1 - R_U^2)SST}{n-k-1} $$

\(SST\) cancels out:

$$ F = \frac{(R_U^2 - R_R^2)/q}{(1 - R_U^2)/(n-k-1)} $$

Overall Significance of a Regression

Econometric software constantly reports an overall \(F\) statistic. This tests the joint hypothesis that all parameters (except the intercept) are zero:

$$ H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \dots, \beta_k = 0 $$

In this case, \(R_R^2 = 0\) (no variables explain \(y\)) and \(q = k\). Thus:

$$ F = \frac{R^2 / k}{(1 - R^2) / (n-k-1)} $$

Testing General Linear Restrictions

Sometimes hypotheses are not just "excluding" variables, e.g., \(H_0: \beta_1 = 1, \beta_2 = 0\).

Example: CAPM Model

$$ r_{i,t} - r_{f,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i(r_{M,t} - r_{f,t}) + u_{i,t} $$

If \(H_0: \alpha_i = 0, \beta_i = 1\) (Efficient Market Hypothesis):

$$ r_{i,t} - r_{f,t} = (r_{M,t} - r_{f,t}) + u_{i,t} $$

This leaves nothing to estimate on the right-hand side! We simply compute:

$$ SSR_R = \sum (r_{i,t} - r_{M,t})^2 $$

Note: For general linear restrictions, we can only use the \(SSR\) form of the \(F\)-test (not \(R^2\)), because the dependent variable often changes between the restricted and unrestricted models.

OLS Asymptotics: Large Sample Inference

Consistency

Unbiasedness cannot always be achieved. Consistency is a minimal requirement: as \(n \to \infty\), the estimator collapses to the true parameter \(\beta_j\).

$$ \text{plim}(\hat{\beta}_j) = \beta_j \quad \text{under MLR.1 through MLR.4} $$

Important: We do not need MLR.5 (homoskedasticity) or MLR.6 (normality) for consistency. For MLR.4, we only need zero correlation \(Cov(x_j, u) = 0\).

$$ \text{plim}(\hat{\beta}_1) - \beta_1 = \frac{Cov(x_1, u)}{Var(x_1)} $$

Asymptotic Normality and Efficiency

Normality of \(u\) (MLR.6) is a very strong assumption. Fortunately, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) comes to the rescue for large samples.

Under MLR.1 through MLR.5 (without MLR.6), as \(n \to \infty\):

$$ \frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{se(\hat{\beta}_j)} \;\stackrel{a}{\sim}\; \mathcal{N}(0, 1) $$

This means we can use standard \(t\) and \(F\) statistics for large samples without assuming normally distributed errors. OLS estimators also become asymptotically efficient (lowest asymptotic variance).

Lagrange Multiplier Test

An alternative to the \(F\) test for multiple exclusion restrictions without assuming MLR.6. It tests if the omitted variables are correlated with the residuals of the restricted model.

6 Steps of the LM Test:
  1. State hypotheses: e.g., \(H_0 : \beta_1 = 0, \beta_2 = 0\).
  2. Estimate the restricted model and save the residuals \(\tilde{u}\).
  3. Run an auxiliary regression: regress \(\tilde{u}\) on all independent variables (restricted + unrestricted) and obtain \(R_{\tilde{u}}^2\).
  4. Compute the test statistic: \(LM = n R_{\tilde{u}}^2\).
  5. Under \(H_0\), \(LM \sim \chi_q^2\) (Chi-square with \(q\) restrictions).
  6. Reject \(H_0\) if \(LM > c\) at the given significance level \(\alpha\).
